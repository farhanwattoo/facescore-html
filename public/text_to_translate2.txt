Trust and usability guide
How Face Score Works: AI Face Analysis Process, Photo Quality, and
          Safe Interpretation
Face score tools can be useful when you understand the process behind
          them. This guide explains detection, landmarks, scoring, and the
          limits of photo-based analysis.

The first step is finding whether a face is present in the image. If
          the face is too small, hidden, blurred, or strongly angled, later
          analysis becomes less stable. Good tools should tell users when the
          input is weak instead of pretending every photo is equally reliable.
Step 2: Landmark and Feature Reading
After detection, software estimates facial landmarks around the eyes,
          nose, mouth, chin, eyebrows, and outline. These points support face
          shape, symmetry, golden ratio, expression, and comparison features.
Landmarks are geometry, not identity. They describe where visible
          points appear in one photo. They cannot fully understand personality,
          emotion, or real-world attractiveness.
A score combines signals such as clarity, symmetry, proportions,
          expression, and lighting. Different tools use different weights, so
          two websites may produce different numbers for the same image.
The useful part is not the number alone. It is the explanation: what
          improved the score, what made it unstable, and what to try next.
Step 4: Reading the Result Safely
Results should be read as estimates. Use them to compare photos under
          similar conditions or to improve camera setup. Do not use them to rank
          people, diagnose health, verify legal identity, or judge self-worth.
Best Photo Setup
Use soft front-facing light.
Avoid heavy filters and face reshaping.
Use a clear, current photo.
Compare more than one image before trusting a pattern.
Why Two Tools Can Give Different Scores
Different systems choose different signals. One tool may weight facial
          symmetry heavily, another may emphasize image clarity, and another may
          include expression or style. This is why a number should never be read
          without knowing the method behind it.
Even the same tool can change its output when the image changes. A
          closer camera can distort proportions, while a softer light can make
          landmarks easier to detect. The process is technical, but the user
          lesson is simple: make the input consistent.
What Happens To Uploaded Images?
Every upload-based page should tell users what the tool does with the
          image. Some features only preview the file in the browser. Other
          features may need server processing. The user experience is stronger
          when that distinction is written near the upload button.
How To Retest Fairly
Take three photos in the same place: neutral expression, slight smile,
          and the angle you normally use online. Compare those instead of mixing
          old photos, filters, and different lighting. Fair testing makes the
          result more useful and less emotional.
